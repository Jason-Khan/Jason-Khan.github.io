I. Poor Man’s Augmented Reality

Setup

We were able to label a 3D coordinate system on a small box.

Keypoints with known 3D world coordinates

Using plt.ginput, we mark top left and bottom right for extracting initial bounding boxes.

Propogating Keypoints to other Images in the Video

After the bounding boxes are established for the first frame, we use cv2.TrackerMedianFlow_create to create trackers for the bounding boxes, and as our video goes on, we update our trackers with the new frames and the trackers update the bounding box location. With bounding boxes, we take the midpoint and see that as a keypoint of our video.

Calibrating the Camera

So far, we have a bunch of keypoints in our video. Our next step is to establish a 3D coordinate system that can be mapped onto these 2D keypoints, and once we have the 3D coordinates, we can find the projection matrix that projects a 3D coordinate to its 2D location on the image.

To recover the 3D-to-2D camera projection matrix, we take apart the p’ = Hp equation and rearranged it into Ax = b format, of which x is a vector of the 11 degrees of freedom. Then I was able to call np.linalg.lstsq to solve for the 11 unknowns.

Projecting a cube in the Scene

With the projection matrix, all there is left is to establish the box’s 3D corner coordinates and call draw() to render our AR box!

Result

We were able to render the box at the second row for stability. Our first row keypoints sometimes disappear from our trackers, and that caused the box’s shape to be very unstable.

II. A Neural Algorithm of Artistic Style

We used VGG-19 as our feature extractor.

We followed the paper to extract content loss after conv4_2 and style loss after conv1_1,conv2_1,conv3_1,conv4_1,conv5_1. We used an Adam optimizer with learning rate of 1e-1. By setting alpha/beta = 2e-11, we are able to retrieve solid results within 2000 epochs.

We also followed Yibin’s advice on piazza and started with the content image instead of white noise. This gave us a performance improvement because our content image is already close to a local minima, and starting from here is a good heuristic in guiding our optimizer and converge faster.

III. Lightfield Camera

Depth Refocusing

Images taken with lightfield cameras can have their focus length adjusted. We achieved this by shifting and averaging images to make certain parts more aligned than other parts, so the aligned parts would be in focus(clear) and the other parts would be blurry. With images’ coordinates shown in the filenames, we can shift an image with respect to the center coordinate, (8, 8). By rolling α(8−x)α(8−x) horizontally and α(8−y)α(8−y) vertically, we can refocus to a specific depth.

Aperture Adjustment

By seletively average only images with in a certain radius, we can achieve varying aperature size. Our implementation is to select all images that satisfy |x−8|<=r|x−8|<=r and |y−8|<=r|y−8|<=r and average them.

Bells and Whistles

Interactive Refocusing

We implemented interactive refocusing by using plt.ginput to take in a coordinate (x, y), and extract a patch of 100x100 with (x, y) as the center. We extract a patch for each image, and loop over αα ~ (-5, 5) with a step of 0.2 to find the minimum SSD between the target patch and the current patch. This way we can find the optimal alphas for each image to focus on a customized point.
